# Triple Constraints Framework

## Purpose
Categorize all relevant factors of a problem into three constraint categories: People (60%), Process (20%), and Technology (20%). This framework grounds the analysis in stakeholder reality and prevents the common mistake of leading with technology solutions.

## Structure

### People (60% weight)
The most important category. Identify all stakeholders — anyone who "holds stakes" in the system.

For each stakeholder group, include:
- Who they are (role, position in the system)
- How many / what proportion (data)
- What their specific stake is
- How they are affected by the problem

Stakeholder types to consider:
- Direct customers/end-users
- Service providers / frontline workers
- Regulatory bodies and government agencies
- Payers (insurance, government programs, etc.)
- Community members indirectly affected
- Internal staff and management
- Suppliers and partners

### Process (20% weight)
The mechanisms, workflows, and operational steps that make the system function (or fail).

For each process element, include:
- What the process is
- Supporting data on how it currently performs
- Where breakdowns occur
- Connection to stakeholder experience

Key process areas:
- Core service delivery processes
- Regulatory and compliance processes
- Information flow and data sharing
- Coordination and handoff processes
- Resource allocation processes
- Emergency and exception processes

### Technology (20% weight)
The tools, systems, and infrastructure that support (or constrain) the process.

Note: Technology is the LEAST important category. It will be replaced, it changes every 6 months. Never lead with tech solutions.

For each technology element, include:
- What the technology is
- Current adoption rates / gaps (data)
- How it supports or hinders the process
- Connectivity and interoperability issues

## Output Format

```
# Triple Constraints Analysis: [Problem Name]

## People (60%)

**[Stakeholder Group 1]**
- [Description with data]

**[Stakeholder Group 2]**
- [Description with data]

[Continue for all identified stakeholders]

## Process (20%)

**[Process Element 1]**
- [Description with performance data]

**[Process Element 2]**
- [Description with performance data]

[Continue for all key processes]

## Technology (20%)

**[Technology Element 1]**
- [Current state with adoption data]

**[Technology Element 2]**
- [Current state with adoption data]

[Continue for all relevant tech]
```

## Data Collection Methods
When data is needed, it can come from:
- Interviews (ask the user about their stakeholders)
- Surveys and published research (web search)
- Benchmarks and industry reports (web search)
- Government and regulatory databases (web search)
- Focus groups (ask the user)

## Critical Rules
- People ALWAYS come first. The game at the highest levels of business has nothing to do with tech.
- When there's no competition in the market, bad processes occur because there's no punishment for them
- Focus on the customer experience and the process the customer follows BEFORE the technology
- Technology is a supporting tool — capability chooses the technology, not the other way around
- Every stakeholder listed should have a data point or proportion attached

## Questions to Ask the User
- "Who are the key stakeholders most directly affected by this problem?"
- "Are there stakeholder groups that have conflicting interests?"
- "What does the core service delivery process look like from the customer's perspective?"
- "What systems or tools does the organization currently rely on?"
