# Strategy Framework

## Purpose
Analyze the strategic positioning of the current system or organization. Strategy is about understanding how the system is positioned today — its unique position, how activities are tailored and integrated, what tradeoffs are being made, and whether there is continuity of improvement. This is an entry-level framework that must be completed before diving into deeper diagnostic frameworks.

## What IS Strategy
Strategy is defined by four components working together:

### 1. Unique Position
- What makes this system/organization's situation distinct?
- What specific barriers, advantages, or conditions define the landscape?
- Include data that quantifies the unique position
- Think about: geographic factors, demographic factors, market position, regulatory environment, access constraints

### 2. Tailored Position
- How has the system adapted (or failed to adapt) its activities to its unique position?
- Are activities streamlined together to form coherent patterns?
- Think about: integrated activity systems (like Southwest's self-baggage + online check-in + fast turnaround = seamless low-cost experience)
- Where do activities conflict or fail to reinforce each other?
- Include data showing how tailoring succeeds or fails

### 3. Clear Trade-Offs
- What is the system explicitly or implicitly choosing to sacrifice?
- Every strategy involves trade-offs — name them clearly
- Examples: "super cheap but terrible quality" or "expensive but comes with great warranty"
- What tensions exist between competing priorities?
- The system cannot be everything to everyone — what has it chosen?

### 4. Continuity of Position with Constant Improvement
- Is the system maintaining a consistent strategic direction while improving?
- Or is it stuck in reactive mode with no improvement trajectory?
- Are improvements incremental and aligned, or scattered one-off fixes?
- Include data on legacy systems, modernization efforts, improvement trends

## What is NOT Strategy
- **Measures**: "We are going to improve sales by 6% every year" — that's a target, not a strategy
- **Best practice adoption**: "We are doing this because it's apparently best practice" — that's imitation, not strategy

## Key Insight Section
Synthesize: How does the current strategic positioning create or worsen the problem? Is the system operating from a coherent strategy, or from reactive maintenance? What does the absence of strategy cost?

## Output Format

```
# Strategy Analysis: [Problem/System Name]

## Unique Position
- [Distinctive condition or barrier with data]
- [Another unique factor with data]

## Tailored Position
- [How activities are (or aren't) adapted to the unique position]
- [Where activity integration succeeds or fails, with data]

## Trade-Offs
- [Trade-off 1: X vs. Y]
- [Trade-off 2: X vs. Y]
- [Trade-off 3: X vs. Y]

## Continuity of Position with Constant Improvement
- [Current improvement trajectory or lack thereof]
- [Evidence of reactive vs. proactive posture, with data]

## Key Insight
[Synthesis paragraph: How the current strategic positioning creates the observed problem]
```

## Critical Rules
- Strategy is about the CURRENT state — describe how the system is positioned now, not how it should be
- Always back claims with data and sources
- Name the trade-offs honestly — vague trade-offs are not useful
- Look for where activities SHOULD reinforce each other but don't
- Distinguish between actual strategy and mere targets/measures/best-practice imitation

## Questions to Ask the User
- "What makes this system or organization's situation unique compared to others in the same space?"
- "Are there activities or processes that are designed to work together as a system?"
- "What is being sacrificed or deprioritized? What trade-offs are being made?"
- "Is there a consistent direction of improvement, or is it mostly reactive fixes?"
